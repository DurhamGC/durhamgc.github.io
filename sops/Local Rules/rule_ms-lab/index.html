    Local Rule: MC-ICP-MS lab

    General rules

    • No unauthorised access to lab suite. Access to the lab suite is controlled via code access. The responsible PI ensures adequate measures are in place to ensure anyone they grant lab access to will be adequately trained/supervised.
    • No unauthorised working in the labs. Before doing any work in the lab, all lab users must (i) complete the Earth Sciences Safety Induction and Fire Safety Training, (ii) read this Local Rules document and all relevant Risk Assessments, and (iii) sign the ‘Induction and Authorisation to work declaration’ to affirm that they have read, understood and agree to abide by the protocols detailed in these documents.
    • Risk assessments must be in place for existing procedures (and completed and approved by an appropriate assessor for any new procedure) before work is carried out.
    • Before working independently (i.e. not under direct supervision) the responsible PI must countersign the above declaration to state the lab user is safe, competent, and adequately trained to work without direct supervision.
    • No lone working. A paired worker (“buddy”) system is in operation in laboratories on the 2nd floor. Pairing must be positive, i.e., each person should be concerned about the other’s safety. For work over long periods, regular checking on the other person is essential.
    • No unauthorised working out of hours. After hours working is permitted on a case-by-case basis and risk assessments must explicitly address out of hours working. Authorisation must be given by the responsible PI (or for student work, the student’s supervisor) on the Induction declaration form to the effect that the lab user is safe and competent to work out of hours.
    • The Nu Plasma manuals must be referred to for any maintenance work.

    Personal Protective Equipment and Safety

    • Outdoor shoes must be taken off in the lobby before the step-over bench, and lab clogs must be worn inside the lab.
    • When handing acids or acidified samples, safety gloves and safety glasses must be worn.
    • When handling components of the mass spectrometer (e.g. cones, lenses, torches, etc.), gloves must be worn.
    • Absolutely no food or drink can be taken into the laboratory or consumed there.

    Hazards

    • Dilute mineral acids (2% HNO3) are used to introduce samples into the mass spectrometer. The relevant RA must be read, and necessary PPE must be used.
    • The mass spectrometer uses high voltages. Hardware restrictions prevent access to high voltage systems while they are operational. Only authorised users (lab managers and technicians) may work on the high voltage systems.
    • Radio Frequency (RF) source power. Hardware restrictions prevent access to RF system while operational. General laboratory users can only access to the RF power switch (ON/OFF). Only authorised users (lab managers and technicians) may work on the RF system.
    • High temperature and UV-emitting plasma is used inside the mass spectrometer. The plasma is contained and the plasma power supply is interlocked and cannot be accessed while operating. The plasma is also shielded to prevent escape of UV radiation.
    • Compressed inert gases1 are used to run the mass spectrometer. This is primarily argon, which is used to feed the plasma. Argon is fed from an external tank. Small amounts of nitrogen are occasionally fed into the introduction system, and compressed air is used to keep some valves open. Inlet gas regulators are checked annually, and the instrument regulators are monitored by instrument software.

    1. The lab air volume of ~110 m3 is, at the time of writing, replaced 28 times per hour, equalling an air volume ~3080 m3 per hour. This means that any catastrophic release of gas would have to be very fast to overcome this system. The gas flows themselves are regulated by software-controlled valves within the mass spectrometer. The valves are kept open by compressed air, so that in the case of a failure, the valves will shut.

      Therefore, even in the highly unlikely double catastrophic failure of the air handling and the gas regulators, and power to the mass spectrometer, the gas valves would still shut.

      The laboratory contains oxygen depletion alarms, monitoring both a low level (in case of Ar release) and high level (in case of N2 release). If oxygen depletion alarms are sounding (alarm at entrance to lab), do not enter, but determine cause of alarm (argon or nitrogen), and contact lab managers. ↩
